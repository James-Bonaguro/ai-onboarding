# Rule 4: Your First Answer Is a First Draft, Not a Final Product

## What Most People Do

They type something into AI. They get a response. They think: "This is okay but not great." Then they close the window and go do it themselves.

That's like asking an analyst to write something, reading their first draft, and firing them instead of giving them feedback.

## What To Do Instead

The first response is never the final product. It's a starting point. The real skill is knowing what to say next.

After every AI response, you have three moves:

| Move | When to use it | What to say |
|---|---|---|
| **Sharpen** | The direction is right but it's too vague or generic | "This is too high-level. Give me specific numbers and examples." |
| **Redirect** | It went in the wrong direction | "You're focused on revenue growth but I care about cash flow sustainability. Start over with that lens." |
| **Push deeper** | The surface answer is fine but you need more | "This is a good start. Now stress-test the third point — what happens if their largest customer represents 40% of revenue and doesn't renew?" |

## Real Example

**Round 1 — You:**
> "Review this covenant package and tell me what's weak."

**Round 1 — AI:**
> Gives you a general list of covenant concerns.

**Round 2 — You:**
> "The leverage covenant looks loose. Walk me through exactly what happens if EBITDA drops 15% — do they trip the covenant or does the calculation method let them hide it?"

**Round 2 — AI:**
> Now gives you a specific scenario analysis with the actual math.

**Round 3 — You:**
> "Good. Now write that as a two-paragraph risk flag I can drop into my IC memo."

**Round 3 — AI:**
> Gives you a polished, copy-paste-ready paragraph.

Three rounds. Maybe 4 minutes total. You went from a generic list to a finished paragraph for your memo.

## The Skill

The people who get the most out of AI aren't better at writing the first message. They're better at the second and third message. They know how to steer.

Most people quit after Round 1. That's like reading the first draft of anything and expecting it to be perfect.

## The One-Sentence Rule

**Never accept the first response as the final product. The real value is in rounds 2 and 3.**
