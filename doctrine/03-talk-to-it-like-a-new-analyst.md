# Rule 3: Talk To It Like You're Briefing a New Analyst

## The Mistake

People Google "best AI prompts" and copy-paste them like magic spells:

> "Act as a world-class financial analyst with 20 years of experience at Goldman Sachs who specializes in..."

This is useless. The AI doesn't become smarter because you told it to pretend to be someone impressive.

## What Actually Works

Talk to it the way you'd brief a sharp new analyst on their first day.

You wouldn't hand a new hire a magic phrase and walk away. You'd say:

> "Here's what we're working on. Here's what I need from you. Here's what matters. Here's what to watch out for. Go."

That's it. That's the entire skill.

## The Formula

Every good AI conversation follows four steps:

| Step | What it means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **What I have** | Give it the raw material — the data, the doc, the context | "I'm looking at a B2B software company. $45M revenue, 85% gross margins, growing 12% YoY." |
| **What I need** | Tell it exactly what to produce | "I need a one-page risk summary for my credit committee." |
| **What to avoid** | Set guardrails so it doesn't waste your time | "Don't give me generic risks. Focus on what specifically threatens debt service coverage." |
| **What to fix** | After the first draft, tell it what to change | "The tone is too academic. Make it sound like something I'd actually present. Also, add a section on customer concentration." |

## Real Example

**Bad (copy-pasted prompt):**
> "Act as a senior credit analyst and analyze this company for me."

**Good (briefing an analyst):**
> "I'm reviewing a deal — mid-market healthcare services company, PE-backed, 4x levered. I'm uploading the CIM. I need you to pull out the three biggest risks to the thesis and explain why the sponsor's EBITDA adjustments might not hold up in diligence. Write it the way I'd present it to my IC — direct, no hedging, specific to this deal."

The second version works because you told it what you have, what you need, what to watch for, and how to write it. That's not "prompt engineering." That's just clear communication.

## The One-Sentence Rule

**Don't search for the perfect prompt. Just tell it what you'd tell a smart person sitting next to you.**
