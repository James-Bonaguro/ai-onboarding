# The First 30 Days

This is the centerpiece of the whole product. Twenty drills, Monday through Friday for four weeks. Most take 15 minutes. A few take 30. None of them require you to stay late.

## How to Use This

**Do them in order.** Each week builds on the one before it. If you skip Week 1 and jump to Week 3 because Week 1 looks too easy, you will produce bad work in Week 3 and blame the AI.

**Do them at your desk during a workday.** These are not homework. They are reps you do in the cracks between meetings. Most of them use real materials from real deals you're already working on. That's the point — you're getting better at the job while you do the job, not adding another thing to your evening.

**Use Copilot if your firm is on Microsoft 365 Copilot. Use Claude or ChatGPT for anything where you'd be uploading materials your firm hasn't cleared for outside tools.** When a drill is specific to one tool, it's labeled.

**Keep a running log.** Open a OneNote page (or a Word doc — whatever) called *AI Drill Log*. After each drill, write two lines: what you tried, and what surprised you. By the end of the 30 days you'll have a personal handbook of what works for *your* style of thinking on *your* kinds of deals. That log is more valuable than the drills themselves.

**One drill per day. That's it.** Don't try to cram three at once. The point is reps spread across days, not a single Saturday binge.

## Week 1 — Context and Specificity

The skill you're building this week: noticing how much context the AI needs and learning how to give it without thinking about it.

### Day 1 — The Two-Prompt Test (15 min)
Pick a topic from a deal you're actively working on. Could be the sponsor's growth thesis, a margin assumption, a covenant package, anything.

Open Copilot (or Claude or ChatGPT). Ask the question two ways, in two separate conversations:

1. **Generic version.** *"What should I look for when evaluating a sponsor's revenue growth assumptions in a CIM?"*
2. **Specific version.** *"I'm reviewing a CIM for a B2B SaaS business with 32% historical revenue CAGR. Sponsor is projecting 28% forward CAGR for five years. The company has 18 customers representing 70% of revenue. Tell me the three most aggressive things about that growth projection and what I should ask in management meetings to test them."*

Read both outputs back-to-back. Notice the difference. Write in your log: *what was the first version missing that the second one had?*

This is the entire game in one drill. Get used to feeling the difference.

### Day 2 — Context Block in Front (15 min)
Open a new conversation. Paste your "About You" block (from the setup guide) at the top, even if your custom instructions are already configured. Then ask a real question from your day.

Compare the answer to one you got last week without context. The point isn't that the answer is necessarily 10x better — it's that you start *feeling* the system warm up to your role.

If you're on Copilot and you set up the OneNote context notebook from the power moves guide, today's drill is to add your firm's investment criteria and your PM's known preferences to that notebook. Then ask Copilot to reference it in a real question.

### Day 3 — The "Pretend I'm Your New Analyst" Frame (20 min)
Pick something you actually need to understand better. A new sub-sector. A specific covenant structure you've seen come up twice and don't fully grasp. The mechanics of a payment-in-kind toggle. Whatever.

Open a new conversation and start with: *"Pretend I'm a new analyst on your team and you're walking me through this for the first time. I want to actually understand it, not just know the definition. Use a real example I'd see in the market."*

Then ask your question. Push back on anything that feels glossed over: *"Slow down — explain that part again, assume I haven't seen it before."*

This is the single best way to learn anything new in private credit. Do it any time you find yourself nodding along in a meeting without actually following.

### Day 4 — The Pushback Drill (15 min)
Take a working thesis you have on a current deal — *"I think this deal is worth pursuing because X"* or *"I'm worried about this credit because Y"* — and paste it into a new conversation.

Then say: *"Find the three weakest parts of this thesis. Don't be polite. Tell me what a skeptical IC member would say first."*

Read the response carefully. At least one of the three pushbacks will be something you hadn't considered. Update your thesis. Save it in your log.

The skill: getting in the habit of asking AI to break your own logic before your IC does.

### Day 5 — Voice Mode (20 min)
Put headphones in. Open Copilot's voice mode (or Claude's voice on mobile, or ChatGPT's voice). Go for a 15-minute walk.

Talk through one of these:
- A deal you're confused about
- A presentation you have to give next week
- A topic from a meeting you didn't fully follow

Don't try to be articulate. Just talk. When you're done, ask the AI to organize what you said into a clear structure.

Most analysts will not do this drill. It feels weird the first time. Do it anyway. By the third time it stops being weird and starts being the most useful tool in the kit.

## Week 2 — Documents and Models

The skill you're building this week: feeding real materials into the AI and getting useful work back.

### Day 6 — Your First CIM Pass (30 min)
Take a CIM you're working through right now. Open Copilot (or Claude — Claude handles long PDFs well, just confirm your firm is okay with it).

Upload the CIM. Then run this exact sequence of prompts:

1. *"Read this CIM and tell me, in five bullets, the sponsor's actual growth thesis. Not the marketing language — the underlying claim."*
2. *"Pull every quantitative claim about market size or market share. List them with the page number and tell me which ones look softest."*
3. *"Find the EBITDA adjustments. List each one with the dollar amount, the rationale given, and your assessment of whether it would survive credit committee scrutiny."*
4. *"What's not in this document that should be?"*

That last question is the most important one. CIMs are sales documents. The interesting story is usually what's missing.

If you're on Copilot and you've set up Notebooks, do this drill inside a Notebook for the deal instead of a one-off chat. Same prompts. The Notebook will hold the context for everything you do with this deal going forward.

### Day 7 — Walk Me Through This Model (30 min) — Excel Copilot
Open an Excel model from a current deal. Click the Copilot button. Run this sequence:

1. *"Walk me through this model. What's the structure, what tabs are doing what, and what are the three or four assumptions that drive everything else?"*
2. *"What's the base case revenue growth assumption, where in the model is it set, and where does that number come from?"*
3. *"Stress this model: drop EBITDA margins by 200bps in years 2-5. Tell me what happens to leverage and DSCR in each year."*
4. *"If you were presenting this model to a credit committee tomorrow, what's the one slide you'd put at the front? Draft it."*

If you're not on Copilot, skip the Excel-specific parts and instead paste a simplified version of your model's assumptions into Claude or ChatGPT and run the same logical sequence in text form.

### Day 8 — Three Sentences (15 min)
Take any internal email, memo, or summary you've written in the last week. Paste it in. Ask: *"Cut this to three sentences for an IC member who has 30 seconds. Don't lose the recommendation or the key risk."*

Read the result. Decide whether it's actually better than what you wrote. Sometimes it will be. Sometimes it'll be worse — and figuring out *why* it's worse will teach you something about how you write.

Then flip the drill. Write a one-line recommendation. Ask the AI to expand it into a full paragraph with the supporting logic. Now you've practiced both directions.

### Day 9 — Covenant Reading (20 min)
Take a real credit agreement (or term sheet) you have access to — could be from a current deal or a closed deal you're allowed to use. Find the financial covenant section. Paste it in.

Ask:
1. *"Translate this covenant package into plain English. What does the borrower actually have to do, and what happens if they don't?"*
2. *"What's borrower-friendly about this and what's lender-friendly?"*
3. *"Compare this to standard market terms for a sponsor-backed unitranche of similar size. Where is it loose and where is it tight?"*

The goal isn't to replace your reading. It's to make you faster and to surface things you'd otherwise miss because you're skimming.

### Day 10 — Customer Concentration Hunt (20 min)
Pick any deal in your portfolio or pipeline with non-trivial customer concentration. Pull up everything you have on the company — CIM, model, management presentation, call notes.

Ask the AI: *"Read all of this. Find every claim about customer concentration. Where do the documents agree and where do they contradict each other? And what's the strongest case the sponsor is making that this concentration is okay, versus the strongest case I'd make in committee that it isn't?"*

This is a synthesis drill. It's also the kind of work that used to take a junior analyst the better part of an afternoon and now takes ten minutes.

## Week 3 — Synthesis and Steering

The skill you're building this week: pushing past the first answer. Most people stop when the AI gives them something usable. The next-level move is to keep going.

### Day 11 — The "Worse, Then Better" Drill (20 min)
Take a draft of something you've already written — an investment memo intro, a portfolio update, anything. Paste it. Ask: *"Make this worse. Make it sound like a junior analyst trying too hard. Then make it better — sharper, more direct, less hedged. Show me both."*

The point of "make it worse" is that the AI will surface bad habits you didn't know you had. The point of "make it better" is that you get a stretch version of your own writing without losing your voice.

### Day 12 — Build Your Own Lightweight Agent (30 min)
You're not going to use Agent Builder yet. You're going to do the rehearsal version: write a context block that turns a fresh chat into a custom specialist you can paste at the top of any conversation.

Write a block called *Deal Screener* that includes:
- Your firm's actual investment criteria (sectors, EBITDA size, leverage range, hold size, geography)
- The three things your firm cares about most in a screen
- The two things that are immediate disqualifiers
- The format you want screen output in

Save it in your OneNote (or wherever you keep notes). Now test it: paste it at the top of a new conversation and feed in the next teaser that lands in your inbox. Ask it to screen.

You just built a v0 agent. When v2 of this product comes around and you spin up a real declarative agent, you're going to start with this block as the spec.

### Day 13 — IC Memo First Draft (30 min)
Take the deal you're closest to writing up. Give the AI everything you have — your notes, the CIM extracts, the model output, your working thesis. Then say:

> *"Draft a first-pass IC memo using this structure: (1) the deal in two sentences, (2) why we like it in three bullets, (3) the three biggest risks with how we get comfortable, (4) what we'd ask management at the next meeting, (5) recommendation. Match my firm's tone — direct, no marketing language, no hedging."*

The output will not be ready to send. It will be 60% of the way there, which means it just saved you four hours. Edit the rest yourself. Notice what you had to fix. That's where your unique judgment lives — and that's where your real value is.

### Day 14 — The Adversarial Reader (20 min)
Take any analytical work product — a memo, a portfolio summary, an industry write-up — and paste it. Then say:

> *"Read this as if you are the most skeptical PM at my firm. The one who has done this for 25 years and has seen every flavor of bad deal. What three questions will they ask me that I haven't answered in this document?"*

Add answers to those three questions to your draft. You just did your own pre-IC dry run.

### Day 15 — Portfolio Monitoring Pass (30 min)
Pick one portfolio company you cover. Pull together the most recent compliance certificate, the most recent quarterly financials, and any news or call notes from the last quarter.

Feed all of it in. Ask:
1. *"Summarize the quarter in five bullets. What's the trajectory, what's the watch-list item, and what changed since last quarter?"*
2. *"Compare the financials to the original investment thesis. Where is the company tracking ahead, and where is it falling behind?"*
3. *"Draft a two-paragraph update I could send to my PM. Direct, no padding. Flag the one thing they should care about most."*

This is one of the highest-leverage uses of AI in private credit. Quarterly updates are repetitive, time-consuming, and exactly the kind of work AI handles well once it has the context.

## Week 4 — Integration

The skill you're building this week: weaving AI into your actual workflow so you stop thinking of it as a separate thing you go to and start thinking of it as part of how you work.

### Day 16 — The Morning Catch-Up (15 min)
Tomorrow morning, before you do anything else, open Copilot in Outlook (or your email client of choice). Ask: *"Summarize everything that came in overnight. Group by deal and by portfolio company. Flag anything that needs a response from me today."*

Then open Copilot in Teams (if you have it). Ask: *"What did I miss in meetings yesterday that I wasn't in? Anything that affects something I'm working on?"*

Do this for three days in a row this week. By Day 3 it will feel obvious and you'll wonder how you used to start your day without it.

### Day 17 — The Pre-Meeting Prep (20 min)
Pick a meeting on your calendar tomorrow. Could be a management call, a sponsor pitch, a portfolio review, an internal IC.

Open a new conversation. Paste:
- The meeting subject and context
- The 3-5 documents that will be referenced (or links if you're in Copilot and they live in SharePoint)
- Your role in the meeting

Ask: *"Build me a one-page prep doc. The four things I should know walking in. The three questions I should be ready for. The one question I should ask that nobody else will."*

Print it. Bring it to the meeting. See how it feels.

### Day 18 — The Industry Primer (30 min)
Pick a sector you don't know well but might cover in the next quarter. Spend 20 minutes building a one-document primer with the AI.

> *"I might be looking at deals in [sector] over the next quarter. Build me a primer that covers: the structure of the industry, the three or four large players, the typical economics and unit economics, how recessions hit it, what private credit lenders typically worry about, and the questions I should always ask in management meetings for a company in this space."*

Then drill down. Pick the part of the primer that surprised you most and ask three follow-up questions. Save the result. The next time a deal in that sector lands on your desk, you've already done the prep work.

### Day 19 — The Sticky Problem (30 min) — Voice Mode
There is a problem at work right now that you've been avoiding. Maybe it's a deal you can't decide on. Maybe it's a portfolio company you're confused about. Maybe it's a memo you don't know how to start.

Headphones in. 20-minute walk. Voice mode on. Talk through it.

Start with: *"I'm going to ramble for ten minutes about a problem I'm stuck on. Don't interrupt me, don't structure it, just let me talk it out. Then when I'm done I'll ask you to help me figure out what I actually said."*

When you're done, ask: *"Okay — pull out the three things I'm actually worried about, the thing I think I should do, and the thing I'm avoiding. Be honest."*

This drill will resolve a real problem in real time. Not metaphorically. Actually.

### Day 20 — The Look-Back (30 min)
Open your AI Drill Log from the last four weeks. Skim it.

Then open a new conversation and paste the whole log in. Ask:

> *"Read all of this. What patterns do you see in how I work and what I struggle with? Where am I getting better, where am I still doing the same generic moves, and what's the one habit I should focus on building over the next 30 days?"*

Read the answer carefully. Argue with it. Update your habits accordingly.

## After the 30 Days

You're not done — you're started. The drills are scaffolding. The point of the scaffolding is that, by the end of it, you don't need it.

What should change after 30 days:

- You stop thinking about "prompts" entirely. You just talk to the thing.
- You catch yourself opening Copilot in moments where last month you would have struggled alone for an hour.
- You produce a first draft of an IC memo in 30 minutes instead of three hours, and it's not noticeably worse than the version you used to grind out.
- The colleagues around you start asking how you got faster.

That last one is the bridge to [`../play/01-why-youre-really-here.md`](../play/01-why-youre-really-here.md). When colleagues start asking, that's when you stop being someone who uses AI and start being the person at your firm who knows how to use it. The play folder is about what you do with that.

Move on to the [end-to-end workflows](../workflows/) when you have a real deal in front of you and want a full walkthrough from CIM to IC memo. Until then, repeat any drill that didn't click the first time. Reps matter more than novelty.
