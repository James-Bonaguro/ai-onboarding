# Why You're Really Here

Let's stop pretending. You didn't pick up this repo because you love AI. You picked it up because someone at your firm — probably your PM, possibly the head of credit, definitely a partner during a hallway conversation — said something like *"we need to be using more AI"* and you realized that vague mandate is going to land on you whether you want it or not.

That mandate is also an opportunity, and almost nobody in your peer group is going to recognize it for what it is. This file is about that.

## The Honest Picture of What's Happening at Your Firm

Your firm has been told by its LPs, by its consultants, by every conference panel since 2024, and by its own founders that it needs to "have an AI strategy." Most firms responded by buying licenses for Microsoft 365 Copilot, sending around an internal memo about it, hosting a 60-minute lunch-and-learn that nobody actually listened to, and then letting everyone figure it out on their own.

What that means for you, in plain terms:

- The tool is paid for. It's already on your laptop.
- Almost nobody at your firm is using it well. Most people opened it once, asked it a generic question, got a generic answer, and concluded it wasn't useful.
- Senior people at your firm *want* to be able to point to someone and say, *"X is the person who actually figured out how to use this."*
- The first analyst or VP who can credibly be that person gets visibility that takes most people three to five years of grinding to earn.

That last point is what this whole folder is about.

## What "Visibility" Actually Means in Private Credit

In a private credit shop, the real coin of the realm is being trusted by the people above you. Not just trusted to do good work — every associate at every shop does good work — but trusted enough to be the person they pull aside when there's a hard call to make. That trust gets converted into the things you actually want: bigger deal allocations, more interesting sectors, faster promotion, and a bigger number on your bonus letter.

The path to that trust is normally slow because the things that earn it — sharp judgment on credits, defended views in IC, instincts about sponsors — take years to develop and are hard to demonstrate from the outside.

AI competence is a shortcut. Not because being good at AI substitutes for being good at credit work — it doesn't — but because being visibly faster, more thorough, and more available than your peers is a signal that registers immediately, and the people above you have specifically been told to look for it. You're being graded on a curve, on a skill nobody at your firm has bothered to develop, with the answer key sitting in this repo.

## How to Make Your AI Wins Visible Without Sounding Like a Hype Guy

The single most common mistake people make when they get good at something new is they tell everyone about it constantly. That backfires fast in finance. Nobody wants to hear about your "productivity stack." The people you're trying to impress have a finely-tuned bullshit detector and they will write you off the second you sound like you're doing AI as an identity.

The move is the opposite. You don't talk about AI. You let your output and your speed do the talking, and then — when someone asks — you tell them in two sentences without making it weird.

### The Four Plays

**Play 1 — Be the Person Who Always Has the Question Pre-Answered.**

In every IC meeting, every deal review, every portfolio update, there's a moment where someone — usually the most senior person in the room — asks a question that nobody has prepped for. Most people in the room mumble. Some look at each other. The person who answers it cleanly gets noticed.

You are now the person who can prep for those questions before the meeting. For every deal review, spend ten minutes the night before with Copilot in your deal notebook asking: *"What are the five questions a senior PM is most likely to ask about this deal that we haven't already addressed in the materials?"* Walk in with the answers in your head. Use them when the moment comes.

That's it. You don't say *"I prepped this with AI."* You just have the answer. Three or four IC meetings of that and your reputation starts shifting.

**Play 2 — Document Your Wins in Numbers, Not Adjectives.**

Keep a running log — same OneNote page is fine — of moments where AI saved you serious time or caught something you would have missed. Not stories. Numbers and outcomes.

Examples:
- *"March 14: Used Copilot Notebooks to do CIM read for Project Aurora. Three hours instead of the usual day. Found three EBITDA adjustments the sponsor's deck buried."*
- *"April 2: Stress-tested the Project Carmine model with Excel Copilot. Caught a circular reference in the sponsor's working capital assumption that would have flowed through to the wrong leverage figure on the IC memo."*
- *"April 8: Drafted the Riverstone quarterly portfolio update in 35 minutes. Last quarter took me 4 hours."*

When your bonus conversation rolls around, you don't argue with adjectives. You point to a list. Senior people respect numbers and respect specifics. Adjectives ("I worked really hard," "I was super focused on AI this year") get ignored. *"I cut my CIM read time by 60% on average over the last six months and here are the specific deals where it mattered"* does not get ignored.

**Play 3 — Become the Quiet Help to People One Level Above You.**

When a VP or principal mentions in passing that they're swamped on a deal, or that they don't have time to read the full data room, that's an opening. Don't push your AI knowledge at them. Just say: *"I can put together a synthesis of the data room in an hour if that helps."* Then go do it. Use Copilot Notebooks to do in 60 minutes what would have taken them six.

You give them the synthesis. You don't ask for credit. You just do it. The next time they need something, they'll come back to you. Three or four of those and you have a senior person actively rooting for your career inside the firm. That is worth more than any single deal.

**Play 4 — Be the Person Junior Analysts Come To.**

When the new analysts join — and they will, every summer and every fall — they're going to be told to "use AI" by the same partners who told you. They'll bounce around for a few weeks, get frustrated, and start asking around for help.

Be the person they ask. Spend 20 minutes walking them through your setup. Point them at this repo. Don't try to teach them everything. Just be visibly generous.

Why this matters: senior people at your firm notice who the junior analysts go to. The associate or VP who is informally mentoring the new class is, by definition, the one who's seen as further along. You don't have to do a single performative thing — just be useful when people ask.

## The Two Things Not to Do

**Don't title yourself.** Don't put "AI Champion" in your email signature. Don't volunteer to "lead the firm's AI initiative" until your reputation is established enough that you can say no to it later. Early formal titles in this space are anchors, not boosters. They make you look like the person who is doing AI *instead of* doing the actual work, which is the opposite of the impression you want.

**Don't try to evangelize peers who aren't asking.** It will cost you social capital and gain you nothing. People who aren't curious about AI yet will become curious on their own timeline once they see your output getting better. Until they ask, focus on your own work. The moment they ask, be helpful and specific. That's the whole protocol.

## What This Looks Like Six Months From Now

If you actually do the drills, work the workflows, and run these plays for a few months, here's what your life looks like:

- You're producing memos that are sharper than what your peers produce, in half the time.
- You're walking into IC meetings with answers prepped for questions nobody else saw coming.
- A VP and a principal at your firm have specifically come to rely on you for synthesis work, even though that's not formally your job.
- The new associates are asking you for help instead of asking each other.
- Your PM has mentioned, in some unprompted moment, that you've been on a tear.
- When the bonus conversation happens, you have a list of specific wins with specific numbers.

None of that requires being a genius. It requires doing the boring practice work in the drills folder, showing up the same way you already do, and letting compounded reps create a gap between you and the people next to you who are still copy-pasting prompts from LinkedIn.

**Get good quietly. Let the work speak. Be useful to the people one level above you and one level below you. The rest takes care of itself.**
